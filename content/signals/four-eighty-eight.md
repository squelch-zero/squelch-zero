# 488

U.S. hospitals currently owned by private equity firms. That's 8.5% of all private hospitals and 22.6% of all proprietary for-profit hospitals in the country.

Texas has 108 of them.

---

# 25.4%

Increase in hospital-acquired conditions after private equity acquisition, compared with matched control hospitals. Falls increased 27.3%. Central line-associated bloodstream infections increased 37.7%.

Surgical site infections doubled — from 10.8 to 21.6 per 10,000 hospitalizations — despite an 8.1% reduction in surgical volume.

Fewer surgeries. More infections. The hospital was doing less work and doing it worse.

---

# 7.0

Additional deaths per 10,000 emergency room visits at private equity-acquired hospitals. A 13.4% increase from the baseline of 52.4 deaths per 10,000.

After acquisition, emergency department salary expenditures dropped 18.2%. ICU salary expenditures dropped 15.9%. Average hospital-wide full-time employees declined 11.6%.

The mechanism is not complicated: acquire the hospital, reduce the staff, patients die at higher rates. The deaths are not the goal. They are the acceptable cost of the goal.

---

# 10%

Higher mortality rate at private equity-owned nursing homes compared with the industry average. After acquisition, frontline caregiver hours decline 3%. Patients are 50% more likely to be prescribed antipsychotics — drugs generally not recommended for older adults because of safety concerns, but cheaper than the staff required to provide adequate care.

---

# $800 million

What Cerberus Capital Management reportedly made from Steward Health Care.

In 2010, Cerberus bought Caritas Christi — a Catholic health system in Massachusetts — for nearly $900 million. Over the next decade, Cerberus and Steward's executives extracted more than $1.3 billion from the company.

In 2020, Cerberus exited. In May 2024, Steward filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy with over $9 billion in liabilities — including $290 million in unpaid employee wages and nearly $1 billion in unpaid bills to vendors.

Five hospitals have closed since. 2,400 employees laid off.

The CEO, Dr. Ralph de la Torre, bought a $40 million yacht. While he bought his yacht, nurses at Steward hospitals reused gloves. A bankruptcy trustee filed a $3.4 billion lawsuit against him in December 2025.

# The mechanism

Private equity does not build hospitals. It acquires them. The acquisition is usually financed with borrowed money — a leveraged buyout. The debt is then placed on the hospital's balance sheet. The hospital must cut costs to service debt that was taken on to purchase it.

The patients whose infections doubled are paying — with their bodies — for the financial engineering that bought the institution that was supposed to heal them.

This is not a secret. The U.S. Senate Budget Committee released a bipartisan report in January 2025 — co-authored by Sheldon Whitehouse (D-RI) and Charles Grassley (R-IA) — documenting the pattern across two PE-owned hospital chains. The committee reviewed over a million pages of documents.

Among the findings: at one facility, a male nurse sexually assaulted at least nine sedated female patients. Investigators found that "financial factors" may have contributed to his continued employment — the cost of replacing a nurse the hospital could not afford to lose, because the money had already gone to service the debt that bought it.

# 23

Private equity-owned hospitals now rated one star by CMS — the lowest possible rating. That number more than doubled from 10 in the previous year. Thirty PE-owned facilities saw their ratings decrease; only 17 improved.

Three Lifepoint Health hospitals — owned by Apollo Global Management — dropped from 3 stars to 1 in a single year. Apollo collects $9.2 million annually in management fees from Lifepoint.

The average star rating for PE-owned hospitals: 2.5 out of 5.

---

# 6,000+

Physician practices now owned by private equity, up from roughly 800 in 2012. The acquisition wave has moved from dentistry and dermatology into primary care, oncology, urology, gastroenterology, and women's health.

80% of physicians are now employed by a hospital system or corporation, up from 60% in 2019.

At least six states enacted new laws in 2025 aimed at curbing private equity's influence in healthcare. No federal legislation has passed.
