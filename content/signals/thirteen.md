# 13 of 13

Federal statistical agencies that lost staff in 2025. Not most. Not the unlucky ones. All of them.

The Bureau of Labor Statistics. The Census Bureau. The Energy Information Administration. NOAA. The CDC's National Center for Health Statistics. The Bureau of Justice Statistics. The Bureau of Economic Analysis. The Bureau of Transportation Statistics. The IRS Statistics of Income. The National Center for Education Statistics. The National Center for Science and Engineering Statistics. USDA's National Agricultural Statistics Service and Economic Research Service. The Social Security Administration's Office of Research.

Every one lost employees. Several lost leadership. Some lost the capacity to do their core work.

---

# 104

Years. The Consumer Price Index had been published every month, without interruption, since January 1921.

That streak ended in October 2025.

The [43-day government shutdown](https://www.bls.gov/cpi/additional-resources/2025-federal-government-shutdown-impact-cpi.htm) that began October 1 halted BLS data collection. October's CPI report — the number the Federal Reserve uses to set interest rates, the number that determines Social Security cost-of-living adjustments for [67 million beneficiaries](https://www.ssa.gov/news/press/factsheets/colafacts.htm), the number embedded in inflation-indexed tax brackets and TIPS bonds — was cancelled.

September prices were "carried forward" as a substitute. [The Richmond Fed called them "phantom figures."](https://www.richmondfed.org/research/national_economy/macro_minute/2025/phantom_figures_missing_data_in_october)

The streak had survived the Great Depression, World War II, the stagflation of the 1970s, the 2008 financial crisis, and a global pandemic. It did not survive 2025.

# 78

Years. The monthly unemployment rate had been published continuously since January 1948.

That streak also broke in October 2025. The household survey — the Current Population Survey, jointly conducted by BLS and the Census Bureau — was not fielded during the shutdown. Unlike employer payroll data, which can be collected after the fact, household survey responses are time-specific. You cannot call someone in November and ask them about their employment status in October. [The data is gone.](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/11/21/fed-wont-get-key-inflation-data-before-next-rate-decision-as-bls-cancels-october-cpi-release.html)

Before the shutdown, BLS had already [suspended CPI data collection in three cities](https://www.npr.org/2025/06/05/nx-s1-5424367/inflation-data-cpi-government-job-shortages) and reduced sampling by approximately 15% across 72 others. One-third of BLS leadership positions were vacant. The Commissioner had been [fired in August](https://www.nbcnews.com/business/economy/trump-orders-firing-bls-commissioner-weak-jobs-report-rcna222531) — two years into a Senate-confirmed four-year term — after BLS issued unflattering payroll revisions.

A second shutdown in January 2026 delayed the jobs report again.

# 30

Years. The USDA food security survey — the only nationally representative measurement of hunger in America, providing both national and state-level data — ran continuously from 1995 to 2024.

[USDA terminated it in September 2025](https://www.usda.gov/about-usda/news/press-releases/2025/09/20/usda-terminates-redundant-food-insecurity-survey), calling the three-decade survey "redundant, costly, politicized, and extraneous." The December 2025 data collection was cancelled. The 2024 report, published in October, is the final update.

No replacement exists. The [Center on Budget and Policy Priorities noted](https://www.cbpp.org/blog/food-insecurity-remained-high-in-2024-administration-ends-data-collection-before-snap-cuts) that the termination came just before SNAP cuts were scheduled to affect millions of households — meaning there would be no measurement of whether those cuts increased hunger.

# 45

Years. NOAA's Billion-Dollar Disaster Database tracked every weather and climate disaster exceeding $1 billion in damages since 1980. A total of [403 events, $2.9 trillion](https://www.climate.gov/news-features/blogs/beyond-data/2024-historic-year-billion-dollar-weather-and-climate-disasters) in cumulative damages. It was terminated in May 2025.

NBC News later reported that the cancelled data would have shown the costliest six months of weather disasters on record. The data existed. It was collected. It was not published.

[Climate Central took over the database in July 2025](https://www.climatecentral.org/climate-services/billion-dollar-disasters), but institutional continuity — the unbroken thread of methodology, staffing, and government authority — is gone.

NOAA's budget was cut 27% overall. The [Office of Atmospheric Research faced approximately 74% cuts](https://www.science.org/content/article/noaa-budget-cuts-threaten-weather-forecasts). More than 1,880 employees were lost.

# 8,000

Government web pages removed or altered since January 2025. Approximately 3,000 datasets taken offline. Across every agency. The [CDC alone lost 3,000+ pages](https://www.cbpp.org/research/poverty-and-inequality/federal-data-are-disappearing-as-statistical-agencies-face-budget). The DOJ removed 1,180+ pages including all state-level crime data and the National Law Enforcement Accountability Database. The Census Bureau removed 3,000 pages of research materials.

The [American Statistical Association](https://www.amstat.org/news-listing/2025/12/10/the-nation-s-data-at-risk-2025-report) issued a formal report in December 2025: "The Nation's Data at Risk."

Others are trying to preserve what they can. [Harvard Law Library](https://lil.law.harvard.edu/blog/2025/02/06/announcing-data-gov-archive/) archived 311,000+ datasets from data.gov. [Restored CDC](https://www.restoredcdc.org) maintains copies of removed pages. University libraries nationwide are running data rescue operations.

But preservation is not collection. You can save the last snapshot of a dataset. You cannot save the next one.

# 0

The number of these losses that are reversible on their own timeline.

Longitudinal data has a specific property: when you stop collecting it, the gap is permanent. You cannot reconstruct October 2025's unemployment rate from November's data. You cannot estimate 2025 food insecurity from 2024 numbers. You cannot measure emissions from facilities that were never required to report.

Each of these surveys and databases existed because someone decided — years or decades ago — that it mattered to know. That counting hunger mattered. That tracking disasters mattered. That measuring what Americans breathe and eat and earn mattered enough to do it consistently, every month or every year, without interruption.

The decision to stop is not a cost savings. The FY2026 budget proposes further cuts: BLS funding for inflation and employment data [reduced approximately 8% each](https://www.friendsofbls.org/updates/2025/11/12/2025-government-shutdown-faqs-on-bls-data). Census Bureau funding [$196 million short](https://www.cbpp.org/research/poverty-and-inequality/federal-data-are-disappearing-as-statistical-agencies-face-budget) of operational need.

What vanishes is not the data. What vanishes is the ability to know.
