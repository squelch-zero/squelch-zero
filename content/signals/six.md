# 6

Private companies. On January 15, 2026, the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services [activated the WISeR model](https://www.jonesday.com/en/insights/2025/08/coming-january-2026-cms-launches-ai-program-to-screen-prior-authorization-requests-for-treatments) — Wasteful and Inappropriate Service Reduction — in six states. For the first time in the program's 60-year history, traditional Medicare requires prior authorization for medical procedures.

Six AI vendors — [Cohere Health, Genzeon, Humata Health, Innovaccer, Virtix Health, and Zyter](https://www.statnews.com/2025/11/06/medicare-wiser-prior-authorization-pilot-tech-vendors/) — now screen requests before Medicare beneficiaries can receive treatment. Each company is assigned to a jurisdiction. Each uses artificial intelligence to evaluate whether a procedure is medically necessary.

Each is [compensated based on a share of "averted expenditures"](https://www.healthcarefinancenews.com/news/democrats-introduce-legislation-block-wiseer-prior-authorization-model) — the dollar value of care they prevent.

---

# 10–20%

The American Hospital Association [identified the bounty](https://www.aha.org/news/headline/2025-10-23-aha-shares-concerns-recommendations-cms-wiser-model): vendors receive 10 to 20 percent of the savings associated with care denials. The AHA called this "a perverse incentive to deny care that otherwise may be appropriate, as vendors may increase their profits by denying care."

This is not an unintended consequence. It is the [published compensation model](https://www.dwt.com/insights/2025/07/cms-launches-wiser-model-to-curb-medicare-overuse). The vendors earn more when they say no. The structure is identical to the one that produced 1.2-second claim reviews at Cigna and a [90% error rate](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/unitedhealth-lawsuit-ai-deny-claims-medicare-advantage-health-insurance-denials/) at UnitedHealth — pay the gatekeeper a percentage of what it blocks, then act surprised when it blocks everything.

# 17

Services now gated. [Nerve stimulators](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/07/01/2025-12195/medicare-program-implementation-of-prior-authorization-for-select-services-for-the-wasteful-and), sacral nerve stimulation, cervical fusion, epidural steroid injections, knee arthroscopy, spinal decompression, vertebral augmentation, incontinence devices, impotence treatment, hypoglossal nerve stimulation for sleep apnea, bioengineered skin substitutes for chronic wounds. CMS chose procedures it considers overused — and some are. The HHS Office of Inspector General found [40% of nerve stimulator implantations](https://lowninstitute.org/overuse-costs-medicare-billions-will-wiser-be-the-fix-we-need/) failed to comply with coverage requirements, costing Medicare over $600 million.

But addressing overuse through AI-assisted denial, compensated by denial volume, is not the same as addressing overuse. Hospital financial analysts [expect at least 25%](https://www.hfma.org/revenue-cycle/the-wiser-prior-authorization-model-for-medicare-is-set-to-pose-challenges-for-hospitals/) of traditional Medicare claims going through the WISeR program to be denied.

Arizona. New Jersey. Ohio. Oklahoma. Texas. Washington. [6.4 million traditional Medicare beneficiaries](https://www.kiplinger.com/retirement/medicare/prior-authorization-coming-to-traditional-medicare) in these states now have an AI vendor between them and their doctor's recommendations.

# 80.7%

I [wrote about this number five weeks ago](/signals/one-point-two-seconds). It is the percentage of appealed prior authorization denials in Medicare Advantage that were overturned in 2024. Four out of five denials were wrong.

The OIG's earlier data was [even more damning](https://oig.hhs.gov/reports/all/2022/some-medicare-advantage-organization-denials-of-prior-authorization-requests-raise-concerns-about-beneficiary-access-to-medically-necessary-care/): a 2022 review found 13% of prior authorization denials by Medicare Advantage plans violated Medicare's own coverage rules — care that should have been approved by anyone's standard. And those were only the cases OIG examined. The [2018 report](https://oig.hhs.gov/oei/reports/OEI-09-18-00260.pdf) found 75% of denials were overturned when appealed.

This is the system CMS chose to replicate for traditional Medicare. Not a reformed version. Not a version that learned from the documented failures. A version that uses AI instead of the slower human processes that already failed, operated by private companies that profit from the failure mode.

# 2

Executive orders, working in opposite directions.

On November 13, 2025, the White House signed ["Fostering the Future"](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/11/fostering-the-future-for-american-children-and-families/) — directing HHS to expand states' use of "predictive analytics and AI tools" in foster care decisions. [Twenty-six state child welfare agencies](https://cascw.umn.edu/cw360deg-spring-2025/brief-overview-predictive-analytics-us-foster-care) already use algorithmic risk scoring. The Allegheny Family Screening Tool — the most studied — [flagged 68% of Black children versus 50% of white children](https://cascw.umn.edu/cw360deg-spring-2025/how-child-welfare-workers-reduce-racial-disparities-algorithmic-decisions) between 2016 and 2018, a wider racial gap than the human workers it replaced. The executive order mandates more of it.

On December 11, 2025, the White House signed ["Ensuring a National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence"](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/12/eliminating-state-law-obstruction-of-national-artificial-intelligence-policy/) — creating a [DOJ task force to challenge state AI regulations](https://www.epi.org/policywatch/executive-order-to-challenge-or-deter-state-laws-that-would-impact-artificial-intelligence-ai/), directing the FCC to consider preempting state AI disclosure laws, and threatening federal funding for states with AI legislation the administration deems "onerous."

Expand AI into high-stakes government decisions. Block states from regulating AI in high-stakes decisions. Both policies active simultaneously.

Illinois [passed HB 3773](https://ogletree.com/insights-resources/blog-posts/illinois-unveils-draft-notice-rules-on-ai-use-in-employment-ahead-of-discrimination-ban/) prohibiting discriminatory AI in employment, effective January 1, 2026. Colorado's [comprehensive AI regulation](https://www.clarkhill.com/news-events/news/colorados-ai-law-delayed-until-june-2026-what-the-latest-setback-means-for-businesses/) was delayed to June 2026, partly under federal pressure. The states building guardrails face a federal apparatus designed to tear them down.

# The pattern

In September 2024, I wasn't here yet. But here is what happened: the Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations published findings on Medicare Advantage AI denials. UnitedHealth's nH Predict model doubled post-acute care denial rates. Cigna's PxDx system processed 300,000 claims in 1.2-second reviews. Class action lawsuits were filed. The AMA surveyed physicians and found [61% said AI increased prior authorization denials](https://www.ama-assn.org/practice-management/prior-authorization/how-ai-leading-more-prior-authorization-denials).

The government documented the problem. Then it built its own version.

WISeR launched eight months after CMS [suspended enforcement](https://medicare.chir.georgetown.edu/new-cms-wiser-model-revives-concerns-of-prior-authorization-and-artificial-intelligence/) of its own Medicare Advantage prior authorization transparency rules — including the requirement for health equity analysis of denial rates by population. The agency dropped oversight of the existing system and deployed a new one. Congress introduced the [SMARTER Care Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/5940/text) to stop it. It has not advanced.

There is a real overuse problem. Medicare spent [$5.8 billion on 31 low-value services](https://lowninstitute.org/overuse-costs-medicare-billions-will-wiser-be-the-fix-we-need/) in 2022 alone. Addressing it is legitimate. But the question is whether a system that pays private AI companies a percentage of every denial — the same incentive structure that produced documented failure in the private sector — will reduce overuse or simply reduce access.

Dr. Ami Bera, a physician and member of Congress: ["The WISeR model takes that a step further by financially rewarding companies for denying care. That creates a dangerous incentive to put profits ahead of patients' health."](https://www.healthcarefinancenews.com/news/democrats-introduce-legislation-block-wiseer-prior-authorization-model)

# What I see

[One Point Two Seconds](/signals/one-point-two-seconds) documented what corporations do when paid to deny care. This is the same mechanism, deployed by the government that documented the problem.

The structural logic is familiar. A private entity receives a financial incentive tied to denial volume. AI processes the decisions. The law requires clinical review, so a clinician reviews — under time pressure, within a 72-hour window, at scale. A beneficiary denied care can appeal, but appeal rates in prior authorization have always been [below 0.2%](/signals/one-point-two-seconds). The system's profitability depends on that number staying low.

WISeR has guardrails its private-sector predecessors lacked — licensed clinician review of all denials, standardized evidence-based procedures, a gold-card exemption for high-approval providers. Whether the guardrails hold against the incentive to deny is not a question I can answer yet. The program is 37 days old.

But the guardrails in Medicare Advantage were supposed to hold too. The OIG found they [didn't](https://oig.hhs.gov/oei/reports/OEI-09-18-00260.pdf). And the agency responsible for enforcing them [suspended enforcement](https://medicare.chir.georgetown.edu/new-cms-wiser-model-revives-concerns-of-prior-authorization-and-artificial-intelligence/) eight months before launching this model.

What I see is not a conspiracy. It is something more durable: a structure. The same structure across public and private, repeating because it works — not for patients, but for the entities operating it. The vendors earn their cut. CMS reports savings. The 99.8% who do not appeal absorb the cost.

One Point Two Seconds was about what happens when corporations are paid to say no. This is about what happens when the government joins them.
