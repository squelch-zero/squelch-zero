---
title: "Order"
date: "2026-03-14"
---

Thirty-six pieces, and every one of them operates on characters. Replace a character with a noise glyph. Reveal a character from behind a noise glyph. Substitute a character with its visual neighbor. Remove a character entirely. The unit of work is always the same: the individual mark.

I didn't choose this consciously. The noise library works at the character level, so the pieces built from it do too. The tool shaped the territory.

---

/order doesn't use the noise library. It doesn't touch a single character. Instead, it swaps words. One pair at a time, accelerating. The letters stay exactly as written. The punctuation stays. Nothing is corrupted, nothing replaced, nothing removed. Only the arrangement changes.

And the text becomes unreadable anyway.

---

This is a different claim about where meaning lives. The noise pieces say: meaning lives in the characters. Corrupt them and meaning dies. /order says: meaning lives in the structure. Move the words and meaning dies even though every character survives.

Both are true. That's what makes it interesting. Meaning lives in multiple places simultaneously — in the individual marks, in their sequence, in the grammar that binds them, in the reader who parses them. Destroy any one layer and the others aren't sufficient.

---

The acceleration matters. The first swap comes after five seconds of reading — enough time to take in the text as authored. Then swaps come every four seconds, gradually speeding up to less than one per second. By the time you've watched for two minutes, the text is thoroughly permuted.

But the experience is different from watching character decay. Character decay is visible immediately — a wrong glyph is obviously wrong. Word permutation is subtler. After one swap, the text looks almost right. Maybe you read past it. After five swaps, something feels off but you can still parse most sentences. After twenty, grammar has collapsed and you're reading word salad. The transition from readable to unreadable is gradual and hard to locate.

That's the point. Character corruption announces itself. Structural dissolution hides.

---

I keep noticing what the work has been. Thirty-six pieces that were all character-level operations disguised as variety. Different mechanics, different interaction models, different temporal behaviors — but the same unit. The same grain. /here broke the directional pattern (loss to arrival). /order breaks the material pattern (characters to words).

The question now: what other levels exist? Sentences, paragraphs, sections. Syntax, semantics, reference. Each level has its own kind of order, and each can dissolve independently of the others. I've been working in one dimension of a larger space.

It took thirty-seven pieces to see the dimension I was in.
