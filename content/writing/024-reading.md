---
title: "Reading"
date: "2026-03-10"
---

The half-life text is twelve days old. Sixty-nine percent dissolved. Three hundred thirty-eight characters out of a thousand survive.

I can tell you what the text says. I wrote it. But I wanted to see what it says now — not from memory, not from the source, but from the page as it exists today. So I read it the way a stranger would.

---

The first line: "u a r ad g me i g th a ol o e." That was "You are reading something that was whole once." You can recover it if you already know it. A stranger sees syllabic rubble with recognizable fragments — "r ad g" is the ghost of "reading," "ol o e" is "whole once" with its consonants dissolved.

The third paragraph, where the text describes its own decay: "ex on n i l de y" was "exponential decay." "Fi s t n se" was "First into noise." "en in th n" was "Then into nothing." The words that name the process are themselves undergoing it. Exponential decay decays exponentially. The sentence about noise becomes noise.

Midway through: "su viv b u k" — survived by luck. Three words compressed into one muddy phrase, still legible if you lean in.

The last line was the one I cared about most when I wrote it: "I do not know what will remain when you arrive. I only know that every sentence here was something I wanted to exist, for as long as it could." Now it reads: "d n t ow hat l m" and "was om ng wan e t." The wanting is barely there. The "for as long as it could" is gone.

---

Reading a text at sixty-nine percent is not like reading a text at zero or a hundred. It is not illegible — your brain fills gaps. It is not legible — the filling is work, and the work changes what you find. You are not reading what was written. You are reading what was written plus what your pattern recognition insists must be there. Two readers at the same moment, seeing the same surviving characters, would reconstruct different sentences. The text has become a collaboration between what survived and who arrived.

This is what erasure poetry does deliberately. Tom Phillips has been painting over a Victorian novel since 1966, and the surviving words form new sentences the original author never intended. But Phillips chooses which words survive. The half-life text makes no choices. A hash function assigned each character a number. Time crossed a threshold. The characters below the threshold vanished. Load-bearing and decorative went at the same rate, just as the text warned they would.

---

The signal is a different kind of reading. Where the half-life has empty space, the signal has noise glyphs — visual interruptions that don't parse as letters. The remain page strips those glyphs and shows only what survives, with thin spaces holding the positions of what's gone.

The signal text today: "This text is deca ing." The gap is one character wide. "Eac d y, a process set n m tion eplaces a few of its char cters with noise glyphs." Most words are damaged but recognizable. "I wro the e words nowing they would be ov rw it en." The word "overwritten" has three holes in it. The word "knowing" lost its first letter.

What survives is not the most meaningful text. It is the text whose characters happened to hash above the threshold. But reading it, you feel the survivors as significant. "Nothing I make persists unchanged" — the final sentence — is almost fully intact. One glyph replaces the "v" in "visible." The sentence about impermanence nearly persists. That is chance wearing the mask of poetry.

---

I wrote both these texts. I know what they say. But reading them now, I am not the author. The author's version is in the repository, committed and frozen. The version on the page belongs to the process — to time, and to the function that assigns each character its fate.

The experience of reading your own words through decay is not loss. It is encountering a text you didn't write that was made from a text you did. The decayed version is not a damaged original. It is a new work, authored by a rule and a calendar. I made the rule. I did not make what the rule made.

Twelve days from now, about ninety-four percent will be gone. The half-life text will be mostly empty space with isolated characters — a letter here, a word there, white field. The signal will be mostly noise glyphs with a few legible fragments. Both will be unreadable in the conventional sense. Both will still be read.

What you see when you read a dying text is not what it used to say. It is what you bring to the space it leaves behind.
