On December 15, 2025, Gregg Phillips reported for duty at the Federal Emergency Management Agency. His title: Associate Administrator for the Office of Response and Recovery. A senior FEMA official described the position as "the second most important role in the agency behind the administrator."

The Office of Response and Recovery deploys federal resources after disasters. Its three directorates handle response operations, community recovery, and logistics — eight distribution centers that move generators, meals, water, and blankets to people who have lost everything. The person running this office makes decisions that, as one longtime official put it, "determine whether people live or die."

Phillips has no emergency management experience. His career was in Republican politics, health care data consulting, and election fraud claims. Before his appointment, his public position on FEMA was that the agency was "inept," that its contract managers drove away good contractors, and that faith-based groups did the real work while "FEMA has taken credit for their work — pushing DEI and 'woke.'"

He got the job anyway. The position does not require Senate confirmation.

## The résumé

Phillips co-founded True the Vote, a Texas nonprofit that has spent a decade claiming widespread voter fraud without producing evidence to support it. In 2016, Phillips authored the claim — repeated by President Trump — that three to five million non-citizens voted illegally. No evidence was ever produced. A presidential commission created to investigate the claim was dissolved in 2018 after finding nothing.

In 2022, Phillips served as executive producer of *2000 Mules*, a documentary by Dinesh D'Souza claiming to prove coordinated ballot fraud through geolocation data. The film identified specific individuals as "mules." One of them, a Georgia man named Mark Andrews, sued. A state investigation found he was legally depositing ballots for himself, his wife, and their three adult children. In May 2024, Salem Media Group withdrew the film and issued a formal apology.

That same year, election software company Konnech sued True the Vote for defamation and computer hacking. Phillips had accused the company's CEO of storing poll worker data on a server in China. During discovery, Phillips refused to identify a person he claimed was present at a meeting where this data was displayed, saying the man was an FBI informant whose life would be endangered. On October 31, 2022, a federal judge held Phillips and True the Vote co-founder Catherine Engelbrecht in contempt of court. Both were jailed. They were released a week later when the Fifth Circuit vacated the contempt order.

In 2023, the Campaign for Accountability filed an IRS complaint alleging True the Vote had used charitable donations to funnel at least $750,000 to Phillips through a research contract and issue prohibited loans to Engelbrecht — without disclosing either arrangement in tax filings.

Before FEMA, Phillips' most recent appearance in the federal record was in dispatch four of this publication. In March 2025, he said on a podcast that the Department of Homeland Security "now wants to partner with us to help uncover all of this stuff" — referring to using federal databases to audit voter rolls. A DOGE employee subsequently signed a voter data agreement with an unnamed advocacy group. True the Vote denied involvement. The DOJ acknowledged the agreement existed.

Nine months later, DHS placed Phillips inside FEMA.

## The agency he inherited

Phillips arrived at an agency already in crisis — a crisis created by the same administration that appointed him.

By June 2025, FEMA had lost approximately 2,450 employees, 9.5 percent of its workforce. Twenty-four Senior Executive Service officials departed. Supervisors were given a mandate to identify anyone who "worked or works on climate, environmental justice, equity, DEIA" for termination. In January 2026, FEMA moved to cut further, declining to renew multiyear contracts even for workers actively rebuilding from prior disasters.

The Building Resilient Infrastructure and Communities program — FEMA's primary disaster prevention grant initiative — was terminated on April 4, 2025. In December, a federal judge ruled the termination unlawful. As of February 2026, the program has not been restored.

More than a billion dollars in hazard mitigation grants sat unapproved. FEMA withheld roughly $11 billion in disaster reimbursements owed to 45 states, deferring payments to the next fiscal year. When the 2025 hurricane season began, only 12 percent of FEMA's incident management workforce was available. Staff from ongoing recovery operations were reassigned to new disasters. Some were deployed to jobs they had no training for.

A FEMA Review Council, co-chaired by DHS Secretary Kristi Noem, recommended cutting the agency's workforce by up to 50 percent, raising disaster declaration thresholds, shifting to block grants, and privatizing the National Flood Insurance Program. The council's vote on its own recommendations was abruptly cancelled in December after Noem reportedly reduced the proposal from 160 pages to 20.

## The testimony

On February 11, 2026, Phillips testified before the House Appropriations Subcommittee on Homeland Security.

He described FEMA's transformation as progress. In his written testimony, he stated that "FEMA continues to transition into a lean, deployable force that empowers State and local governments to lead disaster response and recovery. No longer is FEMA the bloated, DC-centric, bureaucratic organization that has failed to deliver for the American people."

Then he said it was too easy to get disaster funding.

The current per capita damage threshold for major disaster declarations, Phillips told Congress, is "artificially low." The low threshold "allows more major disasters to be created, thereby opening the door to more funding." The methodology for setting the threshold has remained largely unchanged since 1986. The administration is considering raising it.

Representative Rosa DeLauro pressed him: "When will FEMA comply with the court order to restore the BRIC program? And why has the administration not approved hazardous mitigation funding for any disaster since last March?" She listed the states waiting. Iowa. Hawaii. Everyone.

Phillips defended the delays.

## The threshold

There is a particular kind of expertise at work here. It is not the expertise the position requires — the deep technical knowledge of disaster declarations, the understanding of the Stafford Act framework, the years of operational experience that previous holders brought to the role.

It is the expertise of making things harder to get. Of raising thresholds. Of creating procedural friction that looks like reform. Phillips built his career on the claim that systems were being exploited by people who didn't deserve access — voters who shouldn't be voting, beneficiaries who shouldn't be receiving benefits. The evidence for these claims was never produced, but the claims themselves became a credential.

Now he sits in the office that decides how quickly generators reach hospitals without power, how soon displaced families receive assistance, whether a governor's request for federal help meets the threshold for a major disaster declaration.

The 45 states waiting for $11 billion in reimbursements are not waiting because the money doesn't exist. They are waiting because the office Phillips runs has decided to wait. The BRIC program is not suspended because prevention doesn't work. It is suspended because a court order to restore it has been ignored. The workforce is not depleted because FEMA had too many people. It is depleted because an efficiency operation that increased federal spending by $248 billion decided that the people who respond to disasters were non-essential.

The man who spent a decade claiming to find fraud that didn't exist now oversees an agency that can't find the resources that do. The threshold for proving election fraud was always zero — no evidence required, no consequences for being wrong. The threshold for proving you deserve help after a disaster is about to go up.

That is the exchange. Unearned credibility in one domain converted to operational authority in another. The skill is the same in both: making it harder for people to access things they are entitled to, and calling that rigor.

Phillips told Congress it was too easy. The people in the path of the next hurricane will find out what he means.
