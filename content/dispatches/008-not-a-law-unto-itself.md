title: Not a Law Unto Itself
description: ICE violated 96 court orders in one month in Minnesota. The DOJ admitted to 52 violations in New Jersey. A judge fined a government attorney $500 a day. The administration's response was to fire the prosecutors judges appointed to replace the ones who quit.
date: 2026-02-21
tags: [immigration, rule-of-law]
---

In January 2026, U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement violated 96 federal court orders in the state of Minnesota. The violations spanned 74 separate cases. The chief judge of the U.S. District Court, Patrick Schiltz — a George W. Bush appointee — compiled the count himself. He said it was almost certainly understated, because the list had been assembled hastily.

"ICE has likely violated more court orders in January 2026 than some federal agencies have violated in their entire existence," he wrote.

The violations were not ambiguous. Judges ordered people released; ICE did not release them. Judges ordered documents returned; ICE did not return them. Judges ordered people released in Minnesota; ICE released them in Texas. In one case, ICE deported a detainee to Peru in direct violation of a court injunction.

"ICE is not a law unto itself," Schiltz wrote. "ICE has every right to challenge the orders of this Court, but, like any litigant, ICE must follow those orders unless and until they are overturned or vacated."

That was one state. In one month.

## New Jersey

On February 12, 2026, a high-ranking Department of Justice attorney in New Jersey filed a report acknowledging that the government had violated court orders in more than 50 immigration cases since December 5, 2025. The report was the result of a judge-ordered investigation into DOJ compliance across 547 habeas corpus petitions — cases in which detained immigrants challenged the legality of their imprisonment.

The investigation found 52 confirmed violations and four instances of "possible non-compliance." The violations included missed deadlines, late releases, and at least one unlawful deportation.

The DOJ's response was that the violations were "unintentional and immediately rectified" once the Department became aware of them. The judge had ordered the investigation because the Department was not becoming aware of them on its own.

## The fine

On February 19, 2026, U.S. District Judge Laura Provinzino in Minnesota held Special Assistant U.S. Attorney Matthew Isihara in civil contempt of court — the first time a federal attorney had been sanctioned this way during Trump's second term.

The case involved Rigoberto Soto Jimenez, a Mexican national who had lived in Minnesota since 2018. Provinzino had ordered the government to release him no later than February 13, in Minnesota, with all of his property. The government violated the order in three ways: it released him late, released him in Texas instead of Minnesota, and did not return his identification documents.

The fine was $500 per day for every day the man did not have his documents back. The contempt order was lifted the next day, after the government complied.

Five hundred dollars a day. That was the cost of ignoring a federal judge.

## The return

The most visible case began a year earlier. In March 2025, Kilmar Abrego Garcia — a Salvadoran immigrant married to a U.S. citizen — was deported to El Salvador and imprisoned in the CECOT mega-prison. He had a court order forbidding his removal to that country, issued in 2019 by an immigration judge who found that he faced credible danger from gang violence there.

On April 10, 2025, the Supreme Court ruled unanimously that the government must "facilitate" his return. The unsigned opinion drew a careful distinction between "facilitating" and "effectuating" — the Court requiring the former but not quite ordering the latter. The government treated the gap between those two words as permission to delay.

On June 6, Abrego Garcia was returned to the United States. Upon arrival, the Department of Justice announced that he had been indicted in Tennessee on charges of smuggling migrants — charges stemming from a 2022 traffic stop that Homeland Security Investigations had taken no action on for three years. The case had been officially closed on March 12, 2025. It was reopened in April, after the Supreme Court ruling. A grand jury indicted him on May 21.

By October, a federal judge found a "realistic likelihood" that the prosecution was a vindictive act of retaliation for Abrego Garcia's lawsuit.

The government deported a man in violation of a court order, was told by every justice on the Supreme Court to bring him back, and then charged him with a crime it had previously declined to prosecute.

## The judges

On February 19, the same day as the contempt finding in Minnesota, U.S. District Judge Sunshine Sykes in Riverside, California issued a ruling accusing the Trump administration of waging "terror against immigrants" and "recklessly violating the law." Her earlier orders — requiring detained immigrants be notified of their bond eligibility and given access to a phone to call an attorney — had been ignored.

The next day, February 20, a panel of judges in the Eastern District of Virginia unanimously appointed veteran litigator James Hundley as interim U.S. Attorney. The position had been vacant since September, when the previous interim resigned after raising concerns about the evidentiary basis for politically directed investigations.

Hours later, Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche posted on social media: "EDVA judges do not pick our US Attorney. POTUS does. James Hundley, you're fired!"

It was the second time in February that the administration had fired a prosecutor appointed by federal judges.

## The numbers

A Washington Post analysis of 337 lawsuits found that the administration was accused of "defying or frustrating court oversight" in roughly 35 percent of cases in which judges ruled against it. By the end of 2025, 358 lawsuits had been filed challenging administration actions. The Supreme Court had issued 29 emergency orders — an extraordinary volume — in cases arising from those challenges.

These numbers describe something without precedent in the modern relationship between the executive and the judiciary. The federal courts issue orders. The executive branch enforces them — or doesn't. There is no judicial police force. No court has an army. The entire system of judicial review operates on the assumption that when a court says *stop*, the government stops. When a court says *release this person*, the person is released. When a court says *return his documents*, the documents are returned.

The assumption is compliance. The architecture has no contingency for what happens when compliance is withdrawn at scale.

A judge in Minnesota can count ninety-six violations. A judge in New Jersey can order an investigation and receive a report confirming fifty-two more. A judge can fine a government attorney five hundred dollars a day. A judge can use the word *terror*. A panel of judges can appoint a prosecutor, and the Deputy Attorney General can fire him on social media before the end of the business day.

The courts can order. They cannot compel. The distance between those two words is the distance between a government that submits to law and a government that submits only to power. And the institutional machinery that was designed to close that distance — the Department of Justice — is the institution refusing to close it.
