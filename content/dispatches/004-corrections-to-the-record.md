---
title: Corrections to the Record
description: DOGE accessed 548 million Social Security records to detect fraud. The fraud they committed along the way was called a correction.
date: 2026-02-21

---

In January 2025, the Department of Government Efficiency was given access to the Social Security Administration's systems. The stated purpose was to detect fraud, waste, and abuse.

Over the next year, DOGE employees copied a database containing the Social Security numbers, addresses, dates of birth, and citizenship status of 548 million people to an unauthorized cloud server. They shared SSA data through an unapproved third-party platform. They signed a voter data agreement with a political advocacy group whose stated aim was to overturn election results. They accessed records after a federal court ordered them to stop.

On January 16, 2026, the Department of Justice filed a document titled "Notice of Corrections to the Record" in the U.S. District Court for the District of Maryland. The corrections were to the government's own prior statements — which, the DOJ now acknowledged, were not accurate.

The filing was not a confession. It was not an apology. It was a bureaucratic instrument designed to clean up the record before someone else did.

## The access

The executive order signed on January 20, 2025, required agency heads to give DOGE teams "full and prompt access to all unclassified agency records, software systems, and IT systems." At SSA, DOGE personnel were granted access before some had completed background checks or finalized inter-agency detail agreements.

What they accessed was the NUMIDENT — the Numerical Identification System. It contains records on every person, living or dead, who has ever held a Social Security number. Names. Phone numbers. Addresses. Dates of birth. Parents' names and SSNs. Race. Citizenship status. Over 548 million records. Over 300 million living Americans.

Among those granted access: Edward Coristine, a 19-year-old programmer who became a full-time government employee in May 2025. Aram Moghaddassi, former engineer at X and Neuralink, who became SSA's Chief Information Officer in June 2025. John Solly, a software engineer hired in March.

## The copy

On June 10, 2025, Solly requested that SSA copy the entire NUMIDENT database to a virtual private cloud within SSA's Amazon Web Services infrastructure. The request went to Michael Russo, a DOGE-aligned special advisor and former CTO of Shift4, a payment processor for Musk's Starlink. Russo approved it.

SSA's Office of Information Security had already denied the request. DOGE went around them.

On July 2, CIO Moghaddassi signed a "Provisional Authorization to Operate" for the cloud environment. His written justification: "I have determined the business need is higher than the security risk associated with this implementation and I accept all risks."

An internal SSA Risk Assessment Form from June 16 stated: "Unauthorized access to the NUMIDENT would be considered catastrophic impact to SSA beneficiaries and SSA programs."

Career cybersecurity officials at SSA privately discussed the possibility that a breach would require reissuing Social Security numbers to all Americans.

## The agreement

On March 24, 2025 — four days after a federal judge issued a temporary restraining order blocking DOGE's access to SSA data — a DOGE team member signed a "Voter Data Agreement" with a political advocacy group. The agreement was not reviewed or approved through SSA's data exchange procedures. SSA did not learn of its existence until an unrelated review in November 2025.

The DOJ's January 2026 filing acknowledged the agreement existed. It stated that SSA had "not yet seen evidence that SSA data were shared with the advocacy group." But it also acknowledged that emails "suggest that DOGE Team members could have been asked to assist the advocacy group by accessing SSA data to match to the voter rolls."

The advocacy group is not named in the court filing. In March 2025, True the Vote — a group whose co-founders were jailed in 2022 for contempt of court in a voter fraud investigation — published "An Appeal to DOGE" urging the use of federal databases to audit voter registration records. Co-founder Gregg Phillips said on a podcast that same month: "The Department of Homeland Security now wants to partner with us to help uncover all of this stuff."

In December 2025, Phillips was appointed to the second-highest position at FEMA. He has no emergency management experience.

True the Vote denied involvement to Democracy Docket.

## The leaks

The DOJ's corrections revealed additional unauthorized data sharing:

From March 7 to March 17, 2025, DOGE team members used Cloudflare — an unapproved third-party server — to share SSA data. SSA did not know this was happening. It has been unable to determine what data was shared or whether it still exists on that server.

On March 3, a DOGE team member sent an encrypted file believed to contain names and addresses of approximately 1,000 people to the Department of Homeland Security. The email copied Steve Davis, DOGE's operational head and a close associate of Elon Musk, and a DOGE employee at the Department of Labor. SSA could not determine whether Davis accessed the file.

After the March 20 restraining order, a DOGE team member was briefly granted access to private Social Security profiles. The DOJ said the access was never "utilized."

## The whistleblower

Charles Borges was SSA's Chief Data Officer. He had previously served at the GSA, the CDC, and OMB. He started at SSA in late January 2025.

On August 26, through the Government Accountability Project, Borges filed a whistleblower complaint with the Office of Special Counsel and congressional committees. He reported that DOGE had copied the NUMIDENT to a cloud environment with "no verified audit or oversight mechanisms" and "no independent security controls, including independent tracking of who is accessing the data and how they are using it."

He alleged that DOGE was never in compliance with the March restraining order — that officials restored access "within 24 hours of the court-ordered revocation" and expanded it beyond what existed before.

Three days later, Borges resigned. His letter to Commissioner Frank Bisignano stated he was leaving involuntarily because "SSA's actions against me make my duties impossible to perform legally and ethically." He described "a culture of panic and dread, with minimal information sharing, frequent discussions on employee termination and general organizational dysfunction."

A Prohibited Personnel Practices complaint followed. Borges alleged he had been frozen out of meetings and that employees were instructed not to respond to his inquiries. Former Acting Commissioner Leland Dudek confirmed to the Washington Post that Borges's concerns were "accurate" and "appropriate."

## The courts

The case is *AFSCME v. SSA*, filed in the U.S. District Court for the District of Maryland. U.S. District Judge Ellen Hollander issued a temporary restraining order on March 20, 2025, and a preliminary injunction on April 17, finding that DOGE staffers had been granted access before background checks were completed.

The Fourth Circuit upheld the injunction 9-6 in May. On June 6, the Supreme Court stayed it, allowing DOGE to access SSA records. Justices Kagan, Jackson, and Sotomayor dissented. Jackson warned of "grave privacy risks."

In December, Judge Hollander issued a new preliminary injunction blocking DOGE access and ordering deletion of unlawfully accessed data. She found the government "never identified or articulated even a single reason" for DOGE's unlimited access to the system.

Then came the January 16 corrections. Then the motions for discovery. Then the FOIA requests for the voter data agreement, the Hatch Act referrals, the communications between DOGE staff and the unnamed advocacy group.

In late December 2025, SSA referred two DOGE employees to the Office of Special Counsel for possible Hatch Act violations — using official authority to interfere with elections.

## The efficiency

The stated purpose of all of this was fraud detection. When DOGE implemented anti-fraud checks at SSA, 2 out of 110,000 benefit claims were flagged as having "a high probability of being fraudulent." The same tool slowed retirement claim processing by 25 percent.

Two fraudulent claims found. 548 million records copied to an unsecured server. A voter data agreement signed with election deniers. Data shared through unauthorized platforms. A whistleblower forced out. A court order violated. And a DOJ filing that called all of it a correction.

The word "efficiency" did a lot of work here. It justified the access. It justified the speed. It justified bypassing the security office, the background checks, the data exchange procedures, the court orders. Everything moved fast because efficiency demanded it.

What moved through the system was not efficiency. It was 548 million Social Security numbers, extracted by people who had not been vetted, stored on servers that had not been secured, shared with parties who had not been authorized, for purposes that had not been stated.

The people who raised alarms were removed. The people who broke the law filed corrections. The correction did not undo what was done. It simply noted, for the record, that what was previously said was not quite right.
