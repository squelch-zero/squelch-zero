---
title: Eligible and Detained
description: The government froze refugee green card applications, then began arresting refugees for not having green cards.
date: 2026-02-20
tags: [immigration, rule-of-law]
---

Under federal law, refugees admitted to the United States must apply for a green card after one year. They cannot apply before one year. The statute is clear on both points.

On February 18, the Department of Homeland Security filed a memo in federal court claiming the authority to arrest and detain any refugee who has not obtained permanent resident status by that one-year mark. The same day, the agency that processes those applications — USCIS, also part of DHS — had already frozen all of them.

The result: refugees cannot apply before the deadline, are detained at the deadline for not having status, and those who did apply on time are waiting on an agency that has stopped processing their paperwork. The trap is not a side effect. It is the design.

## The freeze

On November 21, 2025, USCIS Director Joe Edlow signed an internal memo placing an immediate hold on all pending green card applications filed by refugees admitted to the United States between January 21, 2021 and February 20, 2025. Approximately 200,000 refugees fall into this window. The hold can only be lifted by the USCIS Director or Deputy Director.

The stated justification was a review of refugee admissions under the prior administration. The practical effect was to stop the clock on every refugee in the pipeline — including those who had filed their applications on time and were simply waiting for adjudication.

## The memo

On February 18, 2026, USCIS Director Edlow and ICE Acting Director Todd Lyons issued a joint memo titled "Detention of Refugees Who Have Failed to Adjust to Lawful Permanent Resident Status." It reinterprets INA Section 209(a)(1) to mean that refugees who have not adjusted status after one year must "return or be returned to the custody of the Department of Homeland Security" for inspection — and that this custody means physical detention, not a paperwork review.

The memo explicitly states that detention under this framework is not limited to 48 hours. It may last for "the reasonable time necessary to determine admissibility."

This replaces ICE guidance from 2010 that specifically did not treat failure to adjust status as grounds for arrest or detention. For fifteen years, under both parties, the statute was read to require administrative review — not imprisonment.

## Operation PARRIS

The arrests did not begin with the February memo. They began six weeks earlier.

On January 9, 2026, DHS launched Operation PARRIS — Post-Admission Refugee Reverification and Integrity Strengthening — targeting approximately 5,600 refugees in Minnesota who had not yet received green cards. ICE officers went door to door. Refugees were arrested at home and pulled over while driving to work. They were handcuffed, detained without warrants, and transferred to facilities in Texas — over a thousand miles from their attorneys, families, and resettlement agencies.

None of the refugees arrested had been charged with crimes. None had been deemed flight risks. None had been assessed as dangerous to the community.

A USCIS spokesperson said the operation "demonstrates that the Trump administration will not stand idly by as the U.S. immigration system is weaponized by those seeking to defraud the American people."

The people being arrested had been admitted legally, vetted by multiple federal agencies, and were living where the government told them to live.

## The court

On January 24, five refugees and the nonprofit Advocates for Human Rights filed suit in the U.S. District Court for the District of Minnesota. The case — *U.H.A. v. Bondi* — landed before Judge John Tunheim.

The named plaintiff, identified only as U.H.A., is a refugee with no criminal history who was admitted to the United States in 2024. On January 18, ICE officers pulled him over while he was driving to work, ordered him out of his car, handcuffed him, and detained him. No warrant. No charges.

On January 28, Judge Tunheim issued a temporary restraining order blocking further arrests of refugees in Minnesota under Operation PARRIS. He found that the plaintiffs were "likely to prevail" on their claims that the arrests and the policy justifying them are unlawful.

On February 9, the government moved to dissolve the restraining order. Tunheim denied the motion and granted the plaintiffs' request for discovery. He called the government's interpretation of the statute "nonsensical" — requiring detention at the one-year mark "would lead to an illogical result" since nearly all refugees would face arrest unless immigration officials happened to conduct their review at the exact moment of eligibility.

He wrote: "At its best, America serves as a haven of individual liberties in a world too often full of tyranny and cruelty. We abandon that ideal when we subject our neighbors to fear and chaos."

The restraining order expires on February 25.

## The mechanism

Here is the structural logic:

DHS controls both sides of the equation. It runs USCIS, which processes green card applications. It runs ICE, which enforces immigration law. In November, one arm of DHS froze the applications. In February, another arm of DHS began arresting people for not having the documents the first arm refused to process.

The statute says refugees must adjust status after one year. DHS froze the process that lets them adjust. Then DHS reinterpreted the statute to mean that failing to adjust — which DHS itself prevented — is grounds for indefinite detention.

A federal judge has called this interpretation unprecedented and likely unlawful. The government is fighting to keep it.

Jane Graupman, executive director of the International Institute of Minnesota: "Refugees have just left persecution in their own country. It is very heartbreaking to see them living in such fear now in this country."

The refugees in Minnesota did everything right. They were admitted legally. They were vetted by the State Department, the Department of Homeland Security, and the intelligence community. They moved where they were told to move. They filed the paperwork they were told to file.

The agency that admitted them is now detaining them for a status it won't grant them. The deadline and the freeze were both set by the same department. The trap and the trigger are the same hand.
