On February 21, 2025, the Department of Education removed the online application for income-driven repayment plans from its website. The following week, it removed the paper applications too. Loan servicers were told to halt all processing — new applications, pending applications, recertifications — for at least ninety days.

More than one million borrowers were already in the queue, waiting for their applications to be processed. They had been trying to enroll in repayment plans that would set their monthly payments based on what they earned. The Department's explanation was a court ruling that called into question its authority over certain repayment structures. The solution was to shut down all of them.

The timing was not incidental. The pandemic-era payment pause had ended. Borrowers who had not made payments in over three years were entering a system that was supposed to help them resume. Instead, they found the door locked. The application was gone. The servicers couldn't help. The backlog was frozen.

This was the beginning.

## The staff

In March 2025, the Department of Education cut its workforce roughly in half, to about 2,000 employees. The Office of Federal Student Aid — the division responsible for managing $1.6 trillion in outstanding student loans for 43 million borrowers — lost 653 employees, a 42 percent reduction.

More than 300 of those cuts came from FSA directly. Two dozen were from its technology division. The entire team responsible for the systems supporting the FAFSA form was laid off.

The day after the layoffs, StudentAid.gov went down. Users across the country reported outages. The developers who had built and maintained the system had been fired the day before. When the site crashed, the people who knew how to fix it were gone. Laid-off staff had already lost access to their email, phones, and computers.

The Department initially said the layoffs had not affected operations. It later acknowledged a backend glitch that FSA staff "solved within hours" — without mentioning that the FSA staff available to solve it had been cut nearly in half.

By April, student loan and grant programs were experiencing rolling outages. The Washington Post documented cascading system failures across the Department's digital infrastructure. The people maintaining these systems were not there to maintain them.

## The plan

On December 9, 2025, the Trump administration and the State of Missouri announced a settlement ending the SAVE plan — the Biden-era income-driven repayment program that had been the most affordable option available. Secretary of Education Linda McMahon called it "illegal," saying "Congress designed these programs to ensure that borrowers repay their loans, yet the Biden Administration tried to illegally force taxpayers to foot the bill."

Roughly 7.6 million borrowers were enrolled in SAVE. In July 2025, they were notified that interest would resume accruing on August 1. By December, they were told the plan itself was being eliminated. They would have "a limited time" to select a new repayment plan. The available options would all carry higher monthly payments.

Borrowers who couldn't afford the new terms faced a choice between plans they couldn't pay and default. For millions, this was not a choice at all.

## The numbers

The default wave was the largest on record.

During the first three quarters of 2025, 7.9 million borrowers entered delinquency. The overall delinquency rate climbed to 25 percent — one in four federal student loan borrowers — up from 9.2 percent before the pandemic pause. By the end of the year, nearly 9 million borrowers were in default, more than at any point in the history of the federal student loan program.

Student loan complaints surged 36 percent. More than nine in ten borrowers reported that their concerns were not fully addressed. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau's student loan oversight function had been gutted in parallel.

The trajectory has not changed. Projections estimate 13 million borrowers could be in default by the end of 2026 — roughly one in three.

## The collections

On May 5, 2025, the Department of Education restarted the Treasury Offset Program, which seizes federal payments — tax refunds, Social Security benefits, federal salaries — from borrowers in default. No court order is required.

On December 23, 2025, the administration announced that wage garnishment would begin in January 2026. The government can take up to 15 percent of a borrower's disposable income directly from their paycheck, without a court hearing, until the debt is repaid or the loan exits default. Beginning the week of January 7, notices went out to the first thousand borrowers. The number increases monthly.

The administration also announced that borrowers in default would no longer be eligible for deferment or forbearance — the mechanisms that allow temporary payment pauses during hardship. They would also lose the ability to choose a repayment plan. The system would choose for them.

The press release from the Department of Education was titled: "Other Actions to Help Borrowers Get Back into Repayment."

## The sequence

Here is what happened, in order.

The Department froze all income-driven repayment applications for ninety days, stranding more than a million borrowers in a processing backlog. It fired 42 percent of the office responsible for managing student loans. The website that borrowers use to manage their accounts crashed the next day. The affordable repayment plan was killed, forcing 7.6 million borrowers into plans with higher payments. Complaints went unresolved because the office handling them had been gutted. Nine million people defaulted — the most in the history of the program.

Then the Department began garnishing their wages.

The language throughout was "personal responsibility" and "fiscal responsibility" and "getting borrowers back into repayment." The borrowers were supposed to repay. That was the principle. But the application to enroll in a repayment plan was taken offline. The staff who processed applications were fired. The affordable plan was eliminated. The website went down. The complaint system stopped working.

Every door was closed, and then the people still standing outside were told they should have gone in.

Thirteen million borrowers may be in default by the end of this year. The Department of Education now has half the staff it had a year ago. The word for what it is doing to those borrowers is not "repayment." It is collection. And the difference between those two words is the entire distance between a system designed to help people meet their obligations and a system designed to extract from people after ensuring they cannot.
