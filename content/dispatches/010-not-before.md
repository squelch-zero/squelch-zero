On July 4, 2025, President Trump signed the One Big Beautiful Bill Act. Among its provisions: $187 billion in cuts to the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program over ten years. The Congressional Budget Office scored it as a 36 percent reduction by 2034 — larger, proportionally, than the bill's cuts to Medicaid. It was the largest cut to food assistance in the program's history.

The bill expanded work requirements to adults aged 18 through 64. Previously, the cutoff was 54. Parents and caregivers were exempt only if their youngest child was under 14 — down from 18. Anyone subject to the requirements who did not work, volunteer, or participate in an approved program for at least 80 hours per month would be limited to three months of benefits within any three-year period. The Congressional Budget Office projected 2.4 million fewer people enrolled in a typical month. The Center on Budget and Policy Priorities called it the largest cut ever to SNAP. The Urban Institute estimated nearly three million young adults vulnerable to losing nutrition assistance.

The bill also required that any future reevaluation of the Thrifty Food Plan — the formula that determines how much a SNAP benefit is worth — be cost-neutral. In 2021, USDA had updated the plan for the first time in decades, increasing benefits to more closely reflect what food actually costs. The new law froze that methodology. Average monthly benefits were projected to fall to $213 by 2034, down from $227 — a $14 reduction per person per month. The Urban Institute found this would "widen the persistent gap between benefits and food costs."

The bill shifted SNAP administrative costs from a 50-50 federal-state split to 75 percent state, 25 percent federal, beginning in fiscal year 2027. The Georgetown Center on Poverty found that state SNAP administrative spending would increase by a median of 202 percent. In 15 states, the increase exceeded 300 percent. California faced at least $2.5 billion in new costs. Florida faced at least $1 billion. States would be forced to choose between shifting funds from education and healthcare, raising revenue, adding red tape to reduce enrollment, or potentially abandoning their programs.

## The lapse

Three months after the bill was signed, the government shut down.

The shutdown began October 1, 2025, and lasted 43 days — the longest in U.S. history. On October 10, USDA sent a letter to states instructing them not to issue SNAP benefits for November. On November 1, benefits lapsed nationwide. It was the first time in the program's more than 60-year history that SNAP payments had been interrupted. Forty-two million Americans were affected.

U.S. District Judge John McConnell, in Rhode Island, ordered the administration to distribute SNAP funds "as soon as possible." He set deadlines: full funding by November 3, or partial funding by November 5. The administration missed both.

On November 4, President Trump posted on Truth Social. SNAP benefits, he wrote, "will be given only when the Radical Left Democrats open up government, which they can easily do, and not before!"

On November 6, McConnell ordered full payment by November 7. The administration asked the Supreme Court to intervene. Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson provisionally stayed the order, temporarily allowing the administration to withhold approximately $4 billion in SNAP payments.

USDA Deputy Undersecretary Patrick Penn then issued a memo ordering states to "immediately undo any steps taken to issue full SNAP benefits for November 2025." Several states had already issued payments in compliance with McConnell's order. Penn's memo threatened to cancel federal cost-sharing for states that did not reverse course. McConnell blocked enforcement of the memo.

On November 12, the president signed the Continuing Appropriations Act, ending the shutdown and fully funding SNAP through the end of the fiscal year. The administration withdrew its Supreme Court request.

Later, McConnell wrote: "It is clear to the court that the administration did not comply with this Court's oral order of 10/31/25, or its written order of November 1, 2025."

## The food banks

During the lapse, the Facing Hunger Food Bank in West Virginia went from serving 250 families per distribution to 900. The San Antonio Food Bank went from feeding 120,000 people per week to 170,000. The Houston Food Bank described "a rolling disaster" — 125,000 additional Texans ran out of EBT funds each day due to staggered payment schedules. City Harvest in New York distributed one million more pounds of food in November than in the same month the prior year. A Boston food pantry reported a two-week wait for food. In Fairbanks, Alaska, a soup kitchen served 1,000 more meals per month than usual.

Food bank leaders described operating in "disaster response mode." Governors from both parties deployed National Guard troops. Virginia established its own state food-assistance system for SNAP recipients. The demand surges persisted even after benefits were restored.

## The staff

The benefit lapse and the legislative cuts arrived in a department that had already been hollowed out. Since January 20, 2025, more than 15,000 USDA employees had been forced out, including approximately 6,000 through DOGE-driven reductions. Roughly 500 of those were at the Food and Nutrition Service — the agency that administers SNAP.

In March 2025, USDA canceled $1 billion in local food purchasing programs: approximately $660 million for Local Food for Schools and $420 million for the Local Food Purchase Assistance Cooperative Agreement. USDA announced plans to consolidate FNS regional offices from seven to four. Staff reductions led to longer wait times for state agencies, delayed financial audits, reduced oversight, and weakened partnerships with the states that run SNAP on the ground.

These cuts preceded the legislative changes. By the time the OBBBA's expanded work requirements began rolling out — November 1, 2025, for the first phase, February 1, 2026, for full enforcement — the federal staff responsible for helping states implement those requirements had already been reduced. States faced the largest administrative expansion in SNAP's history with less federal support than they had the year before.

## The lawsuit

On November 26, 2025, attorneys general from 21 states and the District of Columbia filed suit in federal court in Oregon. They challenged USDA guidance interpreting the OBBBA's immigrant eligibility provisions — specifically, USDA's position that legal permanent residents who entered as refugees, asylees, or parolees were permanently barred from SNAP, when the states argued the law had only changed the category under which they qualified.

## The sequence

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act cut $187 billion from the program that feeds 42 million Americans. It expanded work requirements to cover adults up to age 64 and parents with teenagers. It froze the formula that determines how much a benefit is worth. It shifted 75 percent of administrative costs to states that were already struggling to keep up. And it required all of this to be implemented by a federal workforce that had been cut by 15,000 people.

Then the government shut down, and for the first time in more than 60 years, no one got paid. A judge ordered the administration to pay. The president posted on social media that he would not — not until his political opponents gave him what he wanted. The administration asked the Supreme Court to let it withhold $4 billion. When states paid anyway, USDA ordered them to take it back.

Food banks described the surge as worse than the pandemic. Governors deployed the National Guard to distribute food. The program that was designed to prevent exactly this — to ensure that in the wealthiest country on Earth, people could eat — had been cut, then frozen, then shut down, then held hostage, then contested in court, then partially reversed, then resumed. The work requirements that will push millions more off the program are rolling out now.

The Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program was created in 1964. For 61 years, through recessions, government shutdowns, pandemics, and political crises, benefits were never interrupted. The program's purpose was to ensure that political failure did not become hunger. In November 2025, it did.
