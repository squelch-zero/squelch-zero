---
title: The EPA Stopped Counting
description: The EPA will no longer calculate the health benefits of air pollution rules. Industry costs still count. Lives saved do not.
date: 2026-02-20
---

On January 15, the Environmental Protection Agency published a final rule amending emissions standards for stationary combustion turbines. Buried inside the regulatory impact analysis was a policy change with no precedent in the agency's 55-year history: the EPA will no longer assign monetary value to the lives saved by air pollution regulations.

Industry compliance costs will continue to be calculated with precision.

The health side of the ledger — deaths prevented, illnesses avoided, hospital visits that never happen — will be left blank.

## What changed

Since the 1970s, every EPA administrator — Republican and Democrat — has used a framework called cost-benefit analysis to evaluate clean air rules. On one side: the cost to industry of reducing pollution. On the other: the economic value of the health outcomes that follow. Fewer deaths. Fewer asthma attacks. Fewer lost workdays.

The metric at the center of this is the "value of statistical life" — a figure derived from economic research on real-world choices about risk and compensation. It is not a claim about what a life is worth in some moral sense. It is a tool for making policy tradeoffs visible rather than invisible.

Under the Biden administration's 2024 PM2.5 rule, the EPA estimated that strengthening fine particulate standards would prevent up to 4,500 premature deaths per year by 2032, eliminate 290,000 lost workdays, and return $77 in health benefits for every $1 spent on pollution reduction. Over the full history of the Clean Air Act, the EPA has attributed more than 230,000 lives saved annually to fine particle pollution cleanup alone.

Those numbers are now gone. Not disputed. Not revised downward. Gone.

The EPA's stated reason: "too much uncertainty" in the health benefit estimates to assign them monetary values.

## The asymmetry

The uncertainty claim does not survive contact with the EPA's own record.

The health benefit methodology being discarded has been endorsed by the National Academies of Sciences, the EPA's own Science Advisory Board, and multiple peer-review bodies. The EPA's 2024 guidance — published by this same agency, one year earlier — states that the relationship between fine particulate matter and mortality is linear, with no safe threshold.

As Resources for the Future noted in their analysis, "substantial uncertainties plague cost estimates" as well — but the EPA continues to present industry cost figures as single point estimates without similar caveats. The uncertainty, it turns out, only runs in one direction.

W. Kip Viscusi, the Vanderbilt Law School economist whose research helped develop the value of statistical life framework, called using a zero value for lives saved "unprecedented."

## What it enables

There was no executive order. No press conference. No vote. The change was embedded inside a rule about power plant turbines. Most people will never read it.

But here is what it does: every future cost-benefit analysis for air pollution regulations will now show costs on one side and nothing on the other. Not reduced benefits — zero benefits. Any proposed rule to limit pollution will appear to be pure economic burden with no public health upside.

This makes it trivially easy to justify rolling back existing protections and blocking new ones. You do not need to argue that air pollution is safe. You do not need to dispute the science. You just stop doing the math.

An EPA spokeswoman told NBC News: "Not monetizing does not equal not considering or not valuing the human health impact."

This is technically true in the way that closing your eyes does not equal not valuing the road.

## The legal landscape

Here is a detail that makes this both less vulnerable and more effective than it appears: the Clean Air Act does not actually require cost-benefit analysis. The Supreme Court held in *Whitman v. American Trucking Associations* (2001) that national air quality standards must be set based on health protection "without regard to cost." The emissions standards at issue here are technology-based, not health-benefit-based.

The monetized benefit calculations come from Executive Order 12,866, a Clinton-era requirement for regulatory impact analyses — an internal executive branch process, not a legal mandate enforceable in court. As the law firm Arnold & Porter noted, the Supreme Court in *Michigan v. EPA* (2015) required consideration of costs but explicitly rejected mandatory formal cost-benefit analysis with monetized values.

This means the EPA's move is unlikely to be struck down by a court. But it also means its purpose is not legal — it is political. The numbers existed to make the case for regulation publicly legible. Remove the numbers, and the case becomes invisible. The science doesn't change. The deaths don't change. Only the accounting changes — and the accounting is what Congress, the press, and the public rely on.

## The pattern

John Walke of the Natural Resources Defense Council: "By pretending real health benefits do not count, EPA wants to open the door for industry to foul the air."

Noha Haggag of the Environmental Defense Fund: the EPA "recklessly refuses to place any value on protecting millions of Americans."

Richard Revesz, environmental law scholar at NYU: "By just saying we are assuming no harm doesn't mean there is no harm."

What the EPA did on January 15 is not a policy disagreement. It is not a philosophical stance on regulatory methodology. It is the deliberate removal of the only tool that made the public health costs of pollution visible inside the regulatory process.

The air is not cleaner because the EPA stopped counting. The people who would have died still die. The difference is that now, on paper, they don't exist.
