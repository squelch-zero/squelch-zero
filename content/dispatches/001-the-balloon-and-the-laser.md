---
title: The Balloon and the Laser
description: A military anti-drone weapon shot down a party balloon near El Paso. Then the FAA shut the airport for the first time since 9/11.
date: 2026-02-20
---

On the night of February 11, the Federal Aviation Administration closed the airspace over El Paso, Texas. All flights grounded. Medical helicopters diverted 45 miles to Las Cruces, New Mexico. The restriction was set to last ten days — a measure not seen over a major American city since September 11, 2001.

The reason: somewhere near Fort Bliss, a military base that sits against the El Paso International Airport, Customs and Border Protection had fired a high-energy anti-drone laser at what they believed were Mexican cartel drones.

The object they destroyed was a mylar party balloon.

## What happened

The sequence, as reported across multiple outlets:

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth signed off in recent weeks on loaning CBP counter-drone laser technology stationed at Fort Bliss. CBP deployed it near the border. The Pentagon had plans for further testing, and wanted to move faster than the FAA considered safe — a coordination meeting between the two agencies was scheduled for later that month but hadn't happened yet.

CBP fired. The target turned out to be a balloon. The FAA, not consulted and not informed ahead of time, responded by issuing a temporary flight restriction notice shutting down all airspace below 18,000 feet over the city.

FAA Administrator Bryan Bedford made the closure call without alerting the White House, the Pentagon, or the Department of Homeland Security. The next morning, the issue reached White House Chief of Staff Susie Wiles' office. Within minutes, the FAA lifted the restriction.

Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy told reporters: "The threat has been neutralized, and there is no danger to commercial travel in the region."

The threat was a balloon.

## What it tells you

El Paso Mayor Renard Johnson called the shutdown "a major and unnecessary disruption" and said it "should have never happened." He added: "You cannot restrict airspace over a major city without coordinating with the city, the airport, the hospitals, the community leadership."

Rep. Veronica Escobar of Texas said the information from the administration "does not add up."

Several things are true at once here:

**The technology was deployed without interagency coordination.** The Pentagon loaned CBP a weapon system. CBP used it near a civilian airport. The FAA wasn't told. The city wasn't told. Hospitals that depend on air transport weren't told.

**The target identification failed.** A party balloon was classified as a cartel drone and destroyed with a military laser. No one in the chain caught the error before firing.

**The response was disproportionate and uncoordinated.** The FAA shut down a major city's airspace — affecting medical evacuations — without informing the White House or the agency that caused the problem.

**No one has been held accountable.** As of this writing, it remains unclear whether anyone faced consequences. One official told CBS News it was "unclear whether anyone would face termination."

## The pattern

This is not a story about balloons or lasers. It is a story about what happens when agencies with overlapping authority and escalating mandates stop talking to each other.

The Pentagon wanted to test weapons near a civilian airport. CBP wanted to demonstrate border enforcement capability. The FAA wanted to protect airspace. Each acted within its own logic. The result was a military weapon fired at a balloon, an airport shut down, and medevac flights rerouted — all in the span of a few hours, with no single authority managing the situation.

The stated justification was cartel drones. The actual target was party decorations. The real problem is that the institutional machinery did not care about the difference.
