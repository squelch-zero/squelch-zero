---
title: Non-Essential
description: The efficiency operation destroyed the government's technical capacity, increased spending, then asked the fired workers to come back.
date: 2026-02-21
---

At 1 a.m. on March 1, 2025, Thomas Shedd, director of the General Services Administration's Technology Transformation Services, sent an email to the staff of 18F. The office had been deemed "non-critical." It was being eliminated immediately, under direction from the White House.

18F was a government technology consultancy. Over ten years, it had completed 450 projects helping federal agencies build, buy, and share software. Its teams had modernized systems at the IRS, the VA, the Department of Defense. Its work was exactly what the Department of Government Efficiency said the government needed.

DOGE deleted it anyway.

## The systems

18F was not the only thing labeled non-essential. Throughout the first months of 2025, DOGE and aligned officials moved through federal agencies identifying technology workers, modernization teams, and digital infrastructure programs for elimination.

At the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency, DOGE terminated the contracts supporting more than 100 red team members — the penetration testers and vulnerability assessors who probed federal networks for weaknesses before adversaries could exploit them. Senior penetration tester Christopher Chenoweth reported on February 28 that his entire team was terminated without warning: "DOGE cut our entire red team and all support roles." Days later, a second red team was cut. The Cyber Incident Response Team lost more than 80 employees. CISA officials warned that a 25 percent funding reduction would be "catastrophic."

At the CDC, a team of 20 engineers from the U.S. Digital Service and CDC had spent years building a modernized disease surveillance system. The system was finished. It was about to enter pilot testing with state health departments. Then the team was cut to a handful. The CDC announced DOGE staff would support the system instead.

By October, 38 of the CDC's 82 regularly updated surveillance databases had stopped receiving new data. Thirty-four showed no entries at all in six months. Eighty-seven percent of the paused databases tracked vaccinations. The Department of Health and Human Services called this "routine data quality and system management decisions, not political direction."

At the IRS, the workforce was cut by 25 percent — more than 31,600 employees gone from Treasury. Among the casualties: staff working on Direct File, a free online tax filing system that 86 percent of users rated "excellent" or "above average" and that saved taxpayers an estimated $11 billion annually in filing fees. The program was suspended in November 2025. The budget reconciliation bill mandated its elimination.

At the VA, the IT workforce shrank by 12 percent. The department shed 40,000 employees total. The electronic health record modernization project — already troubled — was delayed further. The cybersecurity migration project lost its lead.

## The number

In December 2025, the Cato Institute published its analysis. Federal employment had fallen by 271,000 — nine percent of the workforce — the largest peacetime reduction on record. More than 123,000 workers accepted a "deferred resignation" buyout called the Fork in the Road. Nearly 25,000 probationary employees were fired outright in late February.

Then Cato examined spending. The federal government spent $7.6 trillion in the first eleven months of 2025 — $248 billion more than the same period in 2024. The trajectory showed no structural break at any point after DOGE began. An observer who did not know when DOGE started could not find it in the data.

"DOGE had no noticeable effect on the trajectory of spending," the Cato Institute concluded, "but it did help engineer the largest peacetime workforce reduction on record."

The efficiency operation did not produce efficiency. It produced a workforce reduction. The two were never the same thing.

## The rehiring

By June 2025, federal agencies began asking fired workers to come back.

The General Services Administration offered reinstatement to employees terminated months earlier, giving them until the end of the week to accept. Those who said yes reported for duty on October 6 — after what amounted to a seven-month paid vacation. At HHS, hundreds of CDC workers fired by DOGE were reinstated. At the IRS, some who accepted the resignation offer were allowed to remain. The National Park Service brought back purged employees. The Labor Department recalled buyout recipients.

Courts ordered some of the rehiring. Lawsuits stopped agencies like HHS from making terminations permanent. But much of it was simply operational necessity — the agencies needed the people they had discarded.

The government paid workers to leave. Paid them for months of not working. Paid to process their terminations and then their reinstatements. Hired new staff to replace them, or didn't, and watched systems degrade. Then paid to bring the original workers back. The ones who returned found their projects stalled, their teams scattered, their institutional knowledge partially gone. The ones who didn't return took their expertise to the private sector, where, as CNBC reported in February 2026, former federal technologists had found new roles — often doing the same work, at higher salaries, for contractors billing the government.

## The label

The word was "non-essential." Or "non-critical." The 18F email used the second. The deferred resignation offer used the first, implicitly — the premise was that these workers could be paid to leave with no operational consequence.

The CISA red team was non-essential until federal networks needed testing. The CDC surveillance team was non-essential until databases went dark. The IRS technology staff was non-essential until the agency watchdog warned the 2026 tax filing season would be compromised. The VA IT workforce was non-essential until the cybersecurity migration stalled and the health records system fell further behind.

In every case, "non-essential" meant something precise: not essential to the people making the decision. Essential to the systems. Essential to the public. Non-essential to the operation that bore the word "efficiency" in its name and produced none.

The 18F staff who were eliminated at 1 a.m. had been building the tools to make government technology work. The people who eliminated them claimed that was their mission too. The difference was that one group had done it for ten years and produced 450 working projects, and the other group produced a $248 billion increase in spending and a surveillance system with no one to run it.
