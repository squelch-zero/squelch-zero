In February 2025, Sahil Lavingia arrived at the Department of Veterans Affairs. He was the founder and CEO of Gumroad, a startup that sells digital products online. He had no medical experience, no government experience, and no background in veterans' services. He had been embedded by the Department of Government Efficiency to find waste.

On his second day, he built an AI tool to review VA contracts and identify which ones to cancel. The tool used outdated, inexpensive AI models. It analyzed only the first approximately 2,500 words of each contract. It hallucinated the values: it concluded that more than a thousand contracts were each worth $34 million, when some were worth as little as $35,000.

The tool was used anyway. Approximately 2,000 contracts were canceled outright and another 14,000 were allowed to expire without renewal. The initial target list of 875 contracts for immediate termination was revised to 585, then to 447, after frontline VHA employees objected. Among the canceled contracts: suicide prevention and mental health treatment, radiology services, cancer care gene sequencing, PACT Act claims processing, burial benefit outreach, and facility maintenance.

"AI is absolutely the wrong tool for this," said Waldo Jaquith, a former Treasury Department IT official. "AI gives convincing looking answers that are frequently wrong."

Lavingia's access was revoked after he gave an interview to a tech publication. He later joined the IRS as a career employee.

## The staffing

The contract cancellations were one part of a larger operation. In fiscal year 2025, the VA lost more than 40,000 employees. Eighty-eight percent came from the Veterans Health Administration — the system that provides direct healthcare to nine million enrolled veterans.

The original DOGE plan called for firing 83,000 employees to return the VA to 2019 staffing levels. That target predated the PACT Act — the 2022 bipartisan law that expanded VA healthcare to millions of veterans exposed to toxic substances during military service. The law required additional staff to serve additional veterans. DOGE's target would have eliminated the workforce Congress had funded to implement the law Congress had passed.

The plan was revised downward — first to 72,000, then to roughly 30,000 through attrition. What actually happened was 40,000 departures. The losses included 3,000 registered nurses, 1,000 physicians, 1,500 scheduling staff, 700 social workers, and nearly 2,000 claims processors. More than 4,500 employees left the Veterans Benefits Administration. Nearly half of the VBA's 50 Regional Office Directors quit or retired in a single year.

In February 2025, the VA fired staff at the Veterans Crisis Line — the 24-hour suicide prevention hotline for veterans. Secretary Doug Collins later said no crisis line personnel had been laid off. The line still has roughly 500 unfilled positions.

A hiring freeze imposed in January 2025 was officially lifted a year later. Strict staffing caps remain. Facility leadership reports denials and severe delays in hiring approvals for all positions.

## The wait

The loss of 1,000 physicians means an estimated 1.2 million veterans lost their VA provider in a single year.

New mental health patients now wait an average of 35 days for an appointment — nearly double the VA's own 20-day standard. In 15 states, the average exceeds 40 days. At the Orange VA Clinic in Connecticut, the wait is 208 days. At a clinic in Ventura, California, the wait reached 121 days after seven of twelve mental health providers left following a return-to-office mandate.

The VA Inspector General found 4,434 severe occupational staffing shortages across all VA medical facilities in fiscal year 2025 — a 50 percent increase over the prior year. Fifty-seven percent of facilities reported severe shortages in psychology. In December 2025, the OIG's Dr. Kroviak told the Senate that the department was "losing clinical staff because morale is going down" — not only among those being fired, but among those who remained. Clinicians were rescinding job applications, citing "increased instability and toxicity" at the department.

## The benefits

Wait times for legacy disability claims appeals grew by nearly 1,000 days, reaching 3,541 days — approximately 9.7 years.

On April 3, 2025, the VA announced it would terminate the Veterans Affairs Servicing Purchase program effective May 1 — the program that had helped 20,000 veterans avoid foreclosure on their homes. At the time of termination, 75,000 veteran borrowers had missed three or more mortgage payments. Up to 58,000 veterans and their families were left vulnerable to foreclosure with no replacement program.

Seventy-five thousand students using the Dependents' Educational Assistance program saw their education payments delayed after the VA botched a system transition in late summer 2025. A 43-day government shutdown in the fall compounded the problem by shuttering the GI Bill hotline.

On February 17, 2026, the VA published an interim final rule requiring medical examiners to factor in the effects of medication when determining disability ratings — meaning veterans whose conditions were well managed by medication could see their ratings reduced. Major veterans organizations condemned the rule within hours. Within 48 hours, Secretary Collins announced it "will not be enforced at any time in the future."

The rule existed for two days. For those two days, it was the official policy of the Department of Veterans Affairs that if your treatment was working, you were less disabled.

## The sequence

A startup CEO with no medical experience arrived at the Department of Veterans Affairs. On his second day, he built an AI tool that used cheap, outdated models and could not read past the first few pages of a contract. The tool hallucinated the dollar values. It was used to cancel contracts covering suicide prevention, cancer care, and the implementation of a law Congress had passed to care for veterans poisoned by burn pits and toxic chemicals. When healthcare workers protested, the target list was cut in half — meaning roughly half the original cancellations had been wrong. The CEO gave an interview and was removed.

Forty thousand employees left. Eighty-eight percent were healthcare workers. The wait for a mental health appointment reached seven months. The wait for a disability appeal reached nearly ten years. A foreclosure prevention program was killed with 75,000 borrowers already behind. A rule to cut disability ratings for veterans whose medication was working was published and abandoned in 48 hours.

The VA Inspector General found staffing shortages had increased 50 percent in a single year. Clinicians were quitting not because they were fired but because they could see what was happening. The AI tool that decided which contracts to cancel could not correctly determine what those contracts cost. The people who built the tool could not correctly determine what those contracts did.

The motto of the Department of Veterans Affairs is adapted from Abraham Lincoln's second inaugural address: "To care for him who shall have borne the battle, and for his widow, and his orphan." The contracts that were canceled, the staff who were cut, the programs that were terminated — those were the instruments through which that care was delivered. What replaced them was a tool that could not read the value of what it was destroying, built by a man who had not yet learned where the building's exits were.
